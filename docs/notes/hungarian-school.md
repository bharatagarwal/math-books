# The Hungarian school of mathematics

## What it was
A small, poor country (under 10 million between the wars) produced an outsized share of the century's great mathematicians and scientists — Erdős, Pólya, von Neumann, von Kármán, Wigner, Szilárd, Teller — enough that they half-jokingly called themselves "the Martians."

The machinery behind it:
- **KöMaL** — a high-school math journal founded in 1894 (by László Rátz, a famous Budapest teacher). It posed hard problems every month and printed students' names and solutions. Almost every Hungarian mathematician credits it. Erdős: "You actually learn to solve problems there."
- **The Eötvös Competition** (1894; later the Kürschák) — a national problem-solving contest, paired with the journal.
- **Elite schools** — Fazekas, the Lutheran (Fasori) Gymnasium, Eötvös Kollégium — and a culture, shaped by Baron Loránd Eötvös, of teaching through problems rather than rote.

The thread: you learn math by wrestling well-chosen problems and building judgment, not by marching through a syllabus. Reps — but reps chosen for difficulty and interest.

## Read
- **George Pólya, *How to Solve It*** — the method of mathematical problem-solving, written down; over a million copies, still in use. (Also *Mathematics and Plausible Reasoning* and *Mathematical Discovery*.)
- **Paul Hoffman, *The Man Who Loved Only Numbers*** — very readable Erdős biography; the easiest way into the culture.
- **Bruce Schechter, *My Brain Is Open*** — another accessible Erdős biography.
- **Hungarian Problem Book** (I–IV, MAA) — the actual Eötvös/Kürschák competition problems, with solutions.
- **István Hargittai, *The Martians of Science*** — the Hungarian émigré scientists (von Neumann, Wigner, Szilárd, Teller, von Kármán).
- **Sarah Constantin, "Hungarian Mathematics Education"** (srconstantin.github.io) — short, modern, analytical take on why it worked.
- Wikipedia "Hungarian mathematics" and the article "A visit to Hungarian mathematics" — overviews with references.

## Watch
- **_N Is a Number: A Portrait of Paul Erdős_** (1993, dir. George Csicsery) — the canonical film; follows Erdős and his collaborators. The one to watch.
- Csicsery's other math documentaries (e.g. *Hard Problems*, on the US IMO team) — adjacent, not Hungary-specific.
- Honest note: there isn't a well-known drama or series about the Hungarian school. It's mostly books plus the Erdős documentary, not dramatizations.
