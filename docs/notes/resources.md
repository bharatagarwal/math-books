# Resources — what each is, accessibility, status

## Using now
- **Lovász & Vesztergombi, *Discrete Mathematics*** — conversational, Hungarian-tradition discovery style. In the reader.
- **MIT, *Mathematics for Computer Science*** — rigor, CS-relevant (state machines / invariants). Primary spine.
- **AoPS** (Counting & Probability, Number Theory) — reps on chosen problems.

## Explored
- **Francis Su, *Mathematics for Human Flourishing*** — not a textbook; almost no equations. Essays tying math to human goods (play, beauty, justice, love), woven with his own life and his letters with Christopher Jackson, an incarcerated man who became his co-author. Reads easily, no math background needed. Euler Book Prize 2021. The "why," warmer and more human than Lockhart. **Well within reach now.**
- **Jeremy Kun, *A Programmer's Introduction to Mathematics*** — programming-first math (graphs, calculus, linear algebra, eigenvalues, optimization, with code). Despite "Introduction," reviewers peg it ~master's level; difficulty ramps, topics jump, no exercise solutions. Directly my lane, but a stretch right now — better as a later / reference book than a starting point.
- **Nielsen & Matuschak — SRS for understanding** — not flashcard drilling of facts. Nielsen's method ("see through a piece of mathematics"): take a real proof, write prompts that break it into its pieces, review them; the understanding comes from the act of picking it apart, and the reviews keep the whole structure in your head long enough that you start to see the result directly. Matuschak's "prompts for understanding": writing a good prompt is itself deep processing. Makes a hard-won intuition *stick* instead of evaporating; the prompt-writing is itself an intuition rep.
- **Strogatz** — see landscape (trade books accessible; textbook hard).
- **Scott Young, *Ultralearning* / MIT Challenge** — see landscape (performative; reference point only).
- **The Princeton Companion to Mathematics** (Gowers, ed.) — a reference/map, not a course; no exercises (same gap as the other expository books), pitched ~strong-undergrad to grad. Use as a companion, not to learn from. Relevant now: Part I essays (how math works); Part III/IV articles for topics already being drilled elsewhere (number theory — Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic, Modular Arithmetic, Euclidean Algorithm; combinatorics; VII.7 Mathematics and Cryptography); Part VIII (VIII.1 The Art of Problem Solving, VIII.6 Advice to a Young Mathematician). Most of Part III/IV is above current level — grow into it.
