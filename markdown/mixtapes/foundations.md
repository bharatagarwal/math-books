# Foundations Mix: pimbook × Mathematical Notation

Two books, different species. Kun's *Programmer's Introduction to Mathematics* is a **course** — narrative, proofs, one application per chapter, meant to be read linearly and slowly. Scheinerman's *Mathematical Notation* is a **phrasebook** — it teaches no mathematics, it just decodes symbols, with history and code demos woven in. Reading the phrasebook cover-to-cover is dry; reading pimbook without it means stumbling on squiggles mid-proof.

The weave: each Scheinerman chapter is a ~30-minute *preflight* read right before the pimbook chapter that uses those symbols. Appetizer, then the meal. Pimbook's short interlude essays (On Pace and Patience, Variable Names, …) ride along after their parent chapter — they're the palate cleansers.

## The path

1. **[Notation: Letters](../reader/#mathematical-notation/1)** — decode $\mathbb{R}$, Greek, blackboard-bold and font conventions before you see them anywhere.
2. **[pim: Our Goal](../reader/#pim/0)** and **[Math has a Culture](../reader/#pim/1)** — Kun's pitch and the cultural ground rules. Read fast.
3. **[pim: Polynomials](../reader/#pim/2)** — the real start: definitions as class definitions, your first existence-and-uniqueness proof, and secret sharing as the payoff. Keep **[Notation: Functions](../reader/#mathematical-notation/6)** open beside it — $f: A \to B$, domain vs codomain vs range, exactly when pim introduces them.
4. **[pim: On Pace and Patience](../reader/#pim/3)** — the interlude. Permission to be slow.
5. **[Notation: Collections](../reader/#mathematical-notation/2)** and **[Notation: Logic](../reader/#mathematical-notation/3)** — the strongest preflight in the whole weave: $\in, \subseteq, \cup, \cap, \forall, \exists$ are precisely the vocabulary the next chapter assumes.
6. **[pim: Sets](../reader/#pim/4)** — then **[Variable Names & Your Brain](../reader/#pim/5)** as the chaser.
7. **[pim: Graphs](../reader/#pim/6)** — no preflight needed; Collections already covered it. **[Subcultures of Mathematics](../reader/#pim/7)** after.
8. **[Notation: Calculus](../reader/#mathematical-notation/8)** and **[Notation: Approximation](../reader/#mathematical-notation/10)** — limits, $dx$, big-O and $\approx$; Approximation pays off again at pim's Big-O interlude later.
9. **[pim: Calculus with One Variable](../reader/#pim/8)** — then **[On Types and Tail Calls](../reader/#pim/9)**.
10. **[Notation: Linear Algebra](../reader/#mathematical-notation/7)** — second-strongest pairing: vectors, matrices, norms, inner products.
11. **[pim: Linear Algebra](../reader/#pim/10)** — then **[Live and Learn Linear Algebra](../reader/#pim/11)**.
12. **[pim: Eigenvectors & Eigenvalues](../reader/#pim/12)** — the notation chapter from step 10 still covers you. **[Rigor and Formality](../reader/#pim/13)** after.
13. **[pim: Multivariable Calculus](../reader/#pim/14)** — Calculus + Linear Algebra notation both already banked. **[The Argument for Big-O](../reader/#pim/15)** rides along here, with Approximation from step 8 as its reference.
14. **[pim: Groups](../reader/#pim/16)** — skim **[Notation: Functions](../reader/#mathematical-notation/6)** again first; groups are functions wearing a trench coat. Finish with **[A New Interface](../reader/#pim/17)**.

## Floaters

Three notation chapters don't pair with pimbook at all:

- **[Notation: Numbers](../reader/#mathematical-notation/4)** — slot it anywhere early; it's the lightest read.
- **[Notation: Probability and Statistics](../reader/#mathematical-notation/9)** — belongs with the MCS/DM probability work, not this mix.
- **[Notation: Geometry](../reader/#mathematical-notation/5)** — skippable until something cites it.

And the back matter — **[Chart](../reader/#mathematical-notation/12)** and the **[Notation Index](../reader/#mathematical-notation/13)** — isn't reading at all; it's the lookup table to keep in a tab while anything else is open.
