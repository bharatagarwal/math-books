# Foundations Mix: pimbook × Mathematical Notation

These two books belong to different species, and that is precisely why they read well together. Jeremy Kun's *A Programmer's Introduction to Mathematics* is a course: it builds narrative momentum, works through proofs at full length, and closes each chapter with an application that justifies the machinery. Edward Scheinerman's *Mathematical Notation* teaches no mathematics at all. It is a phrasebook — a guide to the symbols themselves, decorated in this fork with historical asides and small programs that let you handle each piece of notation before you meet it in the wild.

Read on its own, the phrasebook is dry; nobody sits down to read a dictionary. But read pimbook without it and you will find yourself stalled mid-proof, wondering what a particular squiggle is doing in the middle of an argument you were otherwise following. The remedy is to treat each Scheinerman chapter as a short preparatory read — half an hour, perhaps — taken just before the pimbook chapter that depends on its symbols. The notation arrives in your hands shortly before you need it, and never sooner.

Kun's interludes, the brief essays he places between chapters, are best read exactly where he put them: after their parent chapter, as a rest between climbs.

## The path

1. Begin with **[Notation: Letters](../reader/#mathematical-notation/1)**. It explains the conventions that govern everything to come — why $\mathbb{R}$ wears that double stroke, what the Greek alphabet is doing in mathematics, and how typeface alone carries meaning.

2. Then read **[Our Goal](../reader/#pim/0)** and **[Math has a Culture](../reader/#pim/1)**. These two chapters set out what Kun is trying to do and the cultural terms on which mathematics is written. They are short, and they ask little.

3. **[Polynomials](../reader/#pim/2)** is where the real work begins: a definition treated the way a programmer treats a class definition, a first genuine existence-and-uniqueness proof, and secret sharing as the reward at the end. Keep **[Notation: Functions](../reader/#mathematical-notation/6)** within reach while you read it — pim introduces $f: A \to B$ and the distinction between codomain and range just as the notation chapter explains them.

4. Follow with the interlude **[On Pace and Patience](../reader/#pim/3)**, which makes the case that slowness is the intended speed.

5. Before the next chapter, read **[Notation: Collections](../reader/#mathematical-notation/2)** and **[Notation: Logic](../reader/#mathematical-notation/3)** together. This is the strongest pairing in the whole sequence: the vocabulary they cover — $\in$, $\subseteq$, $\cup$, $\cap$, $\forall$, $\exists$ — is exactly the vocabulary the coming chapter assumes you already have.

6. Now read **[Sets](../reader/#pim/4)**, and afterwards the interlude **[Variable Names & Your Brain](../reader/#pim/5)**.

7. **[Graphs](../reader/#pim/6)** needs no preparation; the Collections chapter has already given you everything it asks for. The interlude **[Subcultures of Mathematics](../reader/#pim/7)** follows it.

8. Before the calculus chapter, read **[Notation: Calculus](../reader/#mathematical-notation/8)** and **[Notation: Approximation](../reader/#mathematical-notation/10)**. The first covers limits and the language of derivatives and integrals; the second covers $\approx$ and big-O, which will earn its keep a second time further down the path.

9. Read **[Calculus with One Variable](../reader/#pim/8)**, then the interlude **[On Types and Tail Calls](../reader/#pim/9)**.

10. **[Notation: Linear Algebra](../reader/#mathematical-notation/7)** is the second of the two essential preparations — vectors, matrices, norms, and inner products, all of which the next three pimbook chapters lean on.

11. Read **[Linear Algebra](../reader/#pim/10)**, followed by the interlude **[Live and Learn Linear Algebra](../reader/#pim/11)**.

12. **[Eigenvectors & Eigenvalues](../reader/#pim/12)** continues directly from the previous chapter, and the notation you banked in step 10 still covers you. The interlude **[Rigor and Formality](../reader/#pim/13)** comes after.

13. **[Multivariable Calculus](../reader/#pim/14)** draws on both of the notation chapters you have already read, calculus and linear algebra at once. The interlude **[The Argument for Big-O](../reader/#pim/15)** sits here, with the Approximation chapter from step 8 as its reference.

14. Finish with **[Groups](../reader/#pim/16)**. It may help to look once more through **[Notation: Functions](../reader/#mathematical-notation/6)** before starting, since group theory is built almost entirely out of careful statements about functions. The closing interlude, **[A New Interface](../reader/#pim/17)**, ends the book.

## What floats free

Three notation chapters have no partner in pimbook. **[Numbers](../reader/#mathematical-notation/4)** is light enough to slot in anywhere early. **[Probability and Statistics](../reader/#mathematical-notation/9)** belongs with the probability work in MCS and Lovász rather than with this sequence. **[Geometry](../reader/#mathematical-notation/5)** can wait until something cites it.

The back matter — the **[Chart](../reader/#mathematical-notation/12)** and the **[Notation Index](../reader/#mathematical-notation/13)** — is not reading material at all. It is the lookup table, and it earns its place as the tab you keep open beside whatever else you happen to be reading.
