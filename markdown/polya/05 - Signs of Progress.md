# Signs of Progress

As Columbus and his companions sailed westward across an unknown ocean they were cheered whenever they saw birds. They regarded a bird as a favorable sign, indicating the nearness of land. But in this they were repeatedly disappointed. They watched for other signs too. They thought that floating seaweed or low banks of cloud might indicate land, but they were again disappointed. One day, however, the signs multiplied. On Thursday, the 11th of October, 1492, "they saw sandpipers, and a green reed near the ship. Those of the caravel *Pinta* saw a cane and a pole, and they took up another small pole which appeared to have been worked by iron; also another bit of cane, a land-plant, and a small board. The crew of the caravel *Niña* also saw signs of land, and a small branch covered with berries. Everyone breathed afresh and rejoiced at these signs." And the next day they sighted land.

You are not crossing an ocean. You are solving a problem. But the experience is the same: you work intensely, you watch for signs that you are getting somewhere, and you need to read those signs honestly. Some will mislead you. Some will save you. This chapter is about telling them apart.

Our undertaking may be important or unimportant, our problem of any kind — when we are working intensely, we watch eagerly for signs of progress as Columbus and his companions watched for signs of approaching land.


## What progress feels like

Progress in solving a problem is not a single event. It is a change in your conception of the problem. At the outset you have the bare statement. By the end you have the solution. Between those two points, your understanding deepens in ways you can sometimes feel before you can articulate.

Polya identified several aspects of this deepening:

**Mobilization.** You extract from memory the facts, definitions, theorems, and formerly solved problems that bear on the current one. The list questions that drive mobilization are the recall questions: *Do you know a related problem? Do you know a theorem that could be useful? Have you seen it before?* Each time one of these questions produces a useful answer, you have mobilized something.

**Organization.** You combine what you have recalled into a structure adapted to the problem at hand. You are not just collecting facts — you are fitting them together. The list questions that drive organization are the structuring questions: *Can you restate the problem? Can you think of a figure? Can you introduce suitable notation?*

**Reconception.** Enriched with recalled material and reshaped by your attempts, the problem looks different to you now than it did at the start. You see new aspects, new connections. Your mode of conception has changed. The list questions that drive reconception are the variation questions: *Can you vary the problem? Can you change the unknown? Can you drop part of the condition?*

**Prevision.** You begin to foresee the shape of the solution — which tools will matter, which steps will come, how the argument will close. The list questions that drive prevision are the anticipation questions: *Can you foresee what the result will look like? Can you check the result?*

These four aspects — mobilization, organization, reconception, prevision — advance together, sometimes steadily, sometimes in jumps. A sudden jump is what people call a bright idea: an abrupt reorganization of your outlook, a confident prevision of the steps ahead.

You cannot force a bright idea. But you can monitor whether the conditions for one are building. The rest of this chapter is about how to do that monitoring — what to watch for, what to trust, what to distrust, and when to step away.


## Clearly expressible signs

There are certain mental operations typically useful in solving problems. If such an operation succeeds, its success is felt as a sign of progress. Having understood this essential point, you can express with some clearness the nature of many signs of progress. All you have to do is read down the list of questions and look at them from this point of view. To each mental operation clearly conceived corresponds a certain sign clearly expressible. The list, appropriately read, lists also signs of progress.

Here are the clearest signs, keyed to the questions you should already be asking yourself:

**A new datum comes into play.** You have been working with some of the data but not all of it. Suddenly you see how to involve a datum you had not yet used. This is a strong sign. In a well-posed problem, every datum matters. If you have not used all the data, you are not done. If a new move brings an unused datum into the picture, that move has something in common with the correct solution.

Consider a chess problem: you must mate in two moves, and there sits a white knight far from the black king, apparently superfluous. After various trials you hit upon a move that brings that knight into play. In a well-constructed chess problem there is no superfluous piece — you have to use all the data. The new move that accounts for the knight looks like the correct move; it might be the correct move.

The same logic applies in counting. Suppose you are counting the subsets of an $n$-element set and you notice that each element contributes an independent binary choice — in or out — and that this accounts for *every* element. You have brought all the data into play at once. That is a very good sign.

**A clause of the condition is satisfied.** The condition of your problem may have several parts. Each part you manage to satisfy is progress. If your candidate formula has a property that the true answer must have, your formula "looks like" the true answer.

If you conjecture that the number of subsets of $\{1, 2, \ldots, n\}$ is $2^n$, you can check: does this give $1$ when $n = 0$? It does — the empty set has exactly one subset (itself). Does it give $2$ when $n = 1$? It does. Each verified case is a clause of the condition satisfied.

**A simpler related problem emerges.** Analogy is one of the main sources of invention. If, while struggling with a problem, a simpler analogous problem presents itself — spontaneously, not by forced search — that is a favorable sign. The simpler problem may serve as a stepping stone or may contain the germ of the method you need.

**Your notation starts to work for you.** When the symbols you chose begin to reveal structure you did not put there deliberately, something is going right. If you represent subsets of $\{1, 2, \ldots, n\}$ as binary strings of length $n$ and then notice that the strings are just the binary representations of $0$ through $2^n - 1$, the notation has done work for you. That is progress.

**The problem becomes easier to state.** If after some work you can restate the problem more simply, more precisely, or in a form closer to problems you have solved before, you have advanced. Going back to definitions often simplifies the statement and opens a path. You have "deflated" the problem.

**You see what kind of argument will close the proof.** Even before you have the details, if you can foresee that the proof will go by induction, or by bijection, or by contradiction, you have a plan outline. Plans are worth more than isolated moves.

**You recall a related problem that was solved before.** This may be the most decisive move of all. If you remember a formerly solved problem that is related to yours — related by sharing the unknown, or the data, or the method — you have a potential bridge to the solution.

**You clearly understand the nature of the unknown.** Simply knowing what kind of object you are looking for — a number, a set, a function, a proof strategy — is itself progress. If you start with a hazy sense of the goal and arrive at a sharp one, you have advanced. Similarly, if you can clearly dispose the various data so that you can easily recall any one, that is progress. If you can visualize the condition as a whole, that may be an essential advance; if you can separate the condition into appropriate parts, that may be an important step forward.

To read such signs no occult science is needed, only a little common sense and a little experience.


## Less clearly expressible signs

Not all signs of progress come labeled. When you work intently, you feel keenly the pace of your progress: when it is rapid you are elated; when it is slow you are depressed. You feel such differences quite clearly without being able to point out any distinct sign.

"It looks good to me," say the unsophisticated. More sophisticated people express themselves with some nuance: "This is a well-balanced plan," or "No, something is still lacking and that spoils the harmony." Behind these primitive or vague expressions there is an unmistakable feeling which you follow with confidence and which leads you frequently in the right direction. If such feeling is very strong and emerges suddenly, you speak of inspiration. People usually cannot doubt their inspirations and are sometimes fooled by them.

What is the nature of these guiding feelings? Since the more clearly expressible signs of progress are connected with the success or failure of certain rather definite mental operations, the less clearly expressible guiding feelings may be similarly connected with other, more obscure mental activities — perhaps activities whose nature is more "psychological" and less "logical." You cannot always say *why* the approach feels right. But the feeling is real, and often correct.

Treat these guiding feelings as you treat the more clearly expressible signs: with serious attention, not with blind obedience. Trust, but keep your eyes open. And never renounce your judgment.

The right response: always follow your inspiration — with a grain of doubt.


## Misleading signs

Not every encouraging feeling is a real sign. Some signs are noise.

**Early fluency.** You set up notation quickly, draw a figure, restate the problem in two ways. You feel productive. But you have not yet engaged the hard part. Fluency in the setup phase is expected and says little about whether you will find the key idea. Do not mistake activity for progress.

**A long calculation that "feels like work."** Computation is sometimes necessary but is never sufficient. If you are deep in algebraic manipulation without a clear reason — without a plan that told you *why* this calculation matters — you may be grinding without advancing. The question to ask: *Why am I doing this particular calculation? What will I learn from the answer?*

**A result that is "almost right."** You conjecture a formula and it works for $n = 1, 2, 3$ but fails for $n = 4$. The temptation is to patch the formula. Sometimes patching works. But often "almost right" means your approach has a structural flaw, and patching will only hide it. Before you patch, ask: *Why did it fail? Is the failure accidental or does it reveal a misunderstanding?*

**Premature conviction.** You guess an answer and immediately believe it. This is the error Polya warned about most forcefully. "Your guess may be right, but it is foolish to accept a vivid guess as a proven truth — as primitive people often do. Your guess may be wrong. But it is also foolish to disregard a vivid guess altogether — as pedantic people sometimes do." The right response to a guess is to *examine* it, not to adopt it or discard it.

**Pattern without mechanism.** You observe a numerical pattern — the first few values are $1, 2, 5, 14, 42$ — and you feel certain the pattern will continue. But unless you can articulate *why* the pattern holds, you have an observation, not understanding. Observation is a fine starting point. It is not a finishing point.

**Ignoring unfavorable signs.** The reverse error: you see signs that your approach is failing and you explain them away. The formula does not work for $n = 4$ but you decide the case is "degenerate." The proof requires an assumption you cannot justify but you press on hoping it will sort itself out. This is the intellectual version of Mr. John Jones (see below) — clinging to a hypothesis against the evidence.

Signs may also be deceptive in a subtler way. Polya confessed: "I once abandoned a certain path for lack of signs, but a man who came after me and followed that path a little farther made an important discovery — to my great annoyance and long-lasting regret. He not only had more perseverance than I did but he also read correctly a certain sign which I had failed to notice." The cost of misreading signs can be real. And in the other direction, you may follow a road cheerfully, encouraged by favorable signs, and run against an unsuspected and insurmountable obstacle.

Yes, signs may misguide you in any single case, but they guide you right in the majority of them. A hunter may misinterpret now and then the traces of his game but he must be right on the average, otherwise he could not make a living by hunting. It takes experience to interpret the signs correctly. Columbus's companions certainly knew by experience how the sea looks near the shore, and so they were able to read the signs suggesting they were approaching land. The expert problem-solver knows by experience how the situation looks and feels when the solution is near. The expert knows more signs than the inexperienced, and knows them better; the main advantage of the expert may consist in such knowledge. An expert hunter notices traces of game and appraises even their freshness or staleness where the inexperienced one is unable to see anything.


## Examine your guess

A guess that arises after you have attentively considered and genuinely understood a problem deserves to be taken seriously. Such guesses "usually contain at least a fragment of the truth although, of course, they very seldom show the whole truth. Yet there is a chance to extract the whole truth if we examine such a guess appropriately."

Many a guess has turned out to be wrong but nevertheless useful in leading to a better one. No idea is really bad, unless you are uncritical. What is really bad is to have no idea at all.

**The case of Mr. John Jones.** Here is what uncritical acceptance looks like outside mathematics. Mr. Jones works in an office. He had hoped for a raise but was disappointed — the salaries of some colleagues were raised but not his. He worried and worried and finally suspected that Director Brown was responsible.

We cannot blame Mr. Jones for conceiving the suspicion. There were indeed some signs pointing to Director Brown. The real mistake was that, after conceiving that suspicion, Mr. Jones became blind to all signs pointing in the opposite direction. He worried himself into firmly believing that Director Brown was his personal enemy and behaved so stupidly that he almost succeeded in making a real enemy of the director.

The trouble with Mr. John Jones is that he behaves like most of us. He never changes his major opinions. He never doubts them, or questions them, or examines them critically — he would especially hate critical examination, if he understood what that meant.

Don't do as Mr. John Jones does. Don't let your suspicion, or guess, or conjecture, grow without examination till it becomes ineradicable. At any rate, in theoretical matters, the best of ideas is hurt by uncritical acceptance and thrives on critical examination.

The lesson is general. In problem-solving, you will form conjectures. Some will feel compelling. The strength of the feeling is not evidence of correctness — it is evidence that the conjecture *fits* your current mental model, which may itself be flawed. The cure is not to suppress conjectures (they are the raw material of discovery) but to examine each one before it hardens into conviction.

**A mathematical example.** You observe by chance that $1^3 + 2^3 + 3^3 + 4^3 = 100 = 10^2$. Interesting. Does it often happen that a sum of consecutive cubes is a perfect square? You compute:

$$1^3 = 1 = 1^2$$

$$1^3 + 2^3 = 9 = 3^2$$

$$1^3 + 2^3 + 3^3 = 36 = 6^2$$

$$1^3 + 2^3 + 3^3 + 4^3 = 100 = 10^2$$

Every time, a perfect square. You notice the sequence $1, 3, 6, 10$ — these are the triangular numbers $\frac{n(n+1)}{2}$. So you guess:

$$1^3 + 2^3 + \cdots + n^3 = \left(\frac{n(n+1)}{2}\right)^2$$

Now *examine* this guess. State it precisely — you just did. Test it against a case you have not yet checked: $n = 5$. The left side is $1 + 8 + 27 + 64 + 125 = 225$. The right side is $(15/2)^2 = 225/4$ — wait, that is wrong. Recheck: $\frac{5 \cdot 6}{2} = 15$, and $15^2 = 225$. It works.

Now ask *why* it might be true. You have shifted from a problem to find (what is the sum?) to a problem to prove (is the conjecture correct?). The natural approach is mathematical induction: verify the base case, then show that if the formula holds for $n$, it holds for $n+1$. The inductive step requires showing that $\left(\frac{n(n+1)}{2}\right)^2 + (n+1)^3 = \left(\frac{(n+1)(n+2)}{2}\right)^2$, which is a routine algebraic verification. The guess, once examined, becomes a theorem.

Examining a guess means three things:

1. **State it precisely.** A precise statement can be tested. A vague feeling cannot.

2. **Test it against cases you have not yet checked.** You found the pattern using $n = 1, 2, 3, 4$. Now try $n = 5$. Better still, try $n = 0$ — boundary cases are the most informative. If the guess survives, your confidence rises. If it fails, you learn something.

3. **Ask why it might be true.** A guess shifts the problem. Before, you had a "problem to find." Now you have a "problem to prove." Can you prove your conjecture? Can you at least see the outline of a proof?

Here is a common sequence in practice:

- You guess a formula.
- You test it: it fails for some case.
- You examine *why* it fails.
- The failure reveals a structural feature you had missed.
- You revise the guess, incorporating the new feature.
- The revised guess works.

**Another example — graph coloring.** You want to determine the minimum number of colors needed to color the vertices of a graph so that no two adjacent vertices share a color. You look at a particular graph, try two colors, and it works for parts of the graph but fails because two adjacent vertices end up the same color. You try three colors and succeed. You guess: three colors suffice for this graph.

Now examine it. State it precisely: "The chromatic number of this graph is $3$." Test it: is there a reason it could be done with two? If the graph contains a triangle (three mutually adjacent vertices), you need at least three colors — that is a structural reason your guess of three cannot be improved to two. And the three-coloring you found proves three suffice. The guess is now a theorem, supported from both sides.

The wrong guess was not wasted. It was a probe. The failure was informative. This is the normal rhythm of discovery, not a sign of incompetence.


## Determination, hope, and the will to persist

It would be a mistake to think that solving problems is a purely intellectual affair; determination and emotions play an important role. Lukewarm determination and sleepy consent to do a little something may be enough for a routine problem. But to solve a serious problem, will power is needed that can outlast bitter disappointments.

Determination fluctuates with hope and hopelessness, with satisfaction and disappointment. It is easy to keep going when the solution seems just around the corner; it is hard to persevere when you do not see any way out. You are elated when your forecast comes true. You are depressed when the way you have followed with some confidence is suddenly blocked.

"Il n'est point besoin d'esperer pour entreprendre ni de reussir pour perseverer." "You can undertake without hope and persevere without success." Thus may speak an inflexible will, or honor and duty. This sort of determination, however, would not do for the scientist, who should have some hope to start with, and some success to go on. You do not take up a problem unless it has some interest; you settle down to work seriously if the problem seems instructive; you throw in your whole personality if there is a great promise. If your purpose is set, you stick to it, but you do not make it unnecessarily difficult for yourself. You do not despise little successes — on the contrary, you seek them: *If you cannot solve the proposed problem, try to solve first some related problem.*

It is necessary to apportion wisely determination to outlook. The emotional texture of problem-solving — the fluctuations of hope and frustration, the small elations when a case checks out, the discouragement when a promising path closes — is not a distraction from the work. It *is* the work. If you had no opportunity to familiarize yourself with the varying emotions of the struggle for the solution, your mathematical education failed in the most vital point.


## Persist, switch, or rest

When progress stalls, you face a decision with three options: persist on the current path, switch to a different approach, or set the problem aside.

There is no algorithm for this decision. But there are heuristics.

**Persist** when you have a plan that has not yet been fully executed, when signs of progress appeared recently, or when you can identify a specific obstacle and see at least the shape of how to overcome it. Polya's Columbus analogy applies: if the signs are multiplying, keep sailing.

**Switch** when you have been on a path for a substantial time with no signs at all, or when a sign that once looked favorable has turned out to be misleading. Switching means choosing a different approach — a different auxiliary problem, a different representation, a different decomposition of the condition. It does not mean giving up on the problem.

An insect tries to escape through the closed windowpane, tries the same hopeless thing again and again, and does not try the next window which is open. A mouse, caught in a trap, tries to squeeze through between two bars, then between the next two bars, then between other bars — it varies its trials, it explores various possibilities. A person is able, or should be able, to vary trials still more intelligently, to explore the various possibilities with more understanding, to learn from errors. "Try, try again" is good advice. The insect, the mouse, and the person all follow it; but if one follows it with more success than the others it is because they *vary* more intelligently.

Why does variation help? You remember things by a kind of mental association — what you have in mind at present tends to recall what was in contact with it at some previous occasion. Varying the problem, you bring in new points, and so you create new contacts, new possibilities of contacting elements relevant to your problem.

There is also a simpler reason. You cannot hope to solve any worth-while problem without intense concentration. But you are easily tired by intense concentration upon the same point. If your work progresses, there is something to do, there are new points to examine, your attention is occupied, your interest is alive. But if you fail to make progress, your attention falters, your interest fades. To vary the problem is to renew the object of concentration and thereby sustain the concentration itself.

The hardest version of switching is to abandon a line of work that has cost you significant effort. Sunk cost is real psychologically, even when it is irrelevant logically. Ask yourself: *If I were starting fresh right now, with everything I currently know, would I choose this path?* If the answer is no, switch.

**Rest** when you have worked hard and are going in circles, when your attention has dulled, or when you have the impression of almost having the idea but not quite. "Take counsel of your pillow" is old advice. But rest only works on problems you have genuinely engaged. "Only such problems come back improved whose solution we passionately desire, or for which we have worked with great tension; conscious effort and tension seem to be necessary to set the subconscious work going."

Before you rest, try to leave the problem in the best state you can. Settle at least some small point. Clarify some aspect. Write down where you are and what you have tried. This gives the subconscious material to work with, and it gives your future self a running start.

> **Schoenfeld's finding.** Alan Schoenfeld, studying how mathematicians and students actually solve problems, found that the difference between experts and novices was often not in the heuristics they knew but in **control** — the ability to monitor their own problem-solving process and make good decisions about when to persist, switch, or abandon a line of attack. Novices would spend twenty minutes on a fruitless computation without pausing to ask whether it was leading anywhere. Experts would stop after a few minutes, reassess, and change course.


## Subconscious work

One evening Polya wished to discuss a certain author with a friend but could not remember the author's name. He remembered a story by the author, a story *about* the author, everything except the name. He tried repeatedly. Nothing came. The next morning, as soon as he thought of the annoyance of the evening before, the name occurred to him without effort.

The phenomenon is common. You work hard on a problem, fail, set it aside. The next morning — or after a few days — the key idea appears, seemingly from nowhere. The nature of the problem matters little: a forgotten word, a difficult clue from a crossword puzzle, the beginning of an annoying letter, or the solution of a mathematical problem may occur in this way.

This is subconscious work. The conscious mind prepares the ground: it understands the problem, mobilizes relevant knowledge, tries and fails along several paths, builds tension. Then the problem drops below the surface. Something continues to process it — rearranging, recombining, testing connections that the conscious mind did not try or did not finish trying. When a fit is found, the result surfaces as a sudden insight.

You cannot command this process, but you can set the conditions for it:

- **Work hard first.** Subconscious work requires conscious preparation. You must have genuinely engaged the problem, not merely glanced at it. "It would be too easy if it were not so; we could solve difficult problems just by sleeping and waiting for a bright idea."

- **Desire the solution.** Lukewarm interest does not sustain subconscious processing. The problems that come back improved are the ones you care about.

- **Know when to stop.** There is a limit beyond which forcing conscious reflection is counterproductive. When you are going in circles, when each attempt feels like the last one wearing a different hat, it is time to stop. "If today will not, tomorrow may."

- **Leave a clean campsite.** Before stopping, write down the state of the problem: what you know, what you have tried, what seems promising, what failed and why. This is not just for the subconscious — it is for the conscious mind that will pick the problem up again.

Consider a crossword puzzle where you need a seven-letter word and the clue is: "Do the walls again, back and forth." You work at it, try various words, get nowhere. You set it aside. The next morning in the shower, the word hits you: REPAPER. "Re-" for "again," "paper" for walls, and the whole thing reads the same backwards and forwards. The subconscious did not just retrieve a word — it recombined the clue's structure ("back and forth" means palindrome) with the semantic content ("do the walls again" means repaper) in a way the conscious mind had failed to unify.

Whatever may or may not be the merits of the theory of subconscious work, the practical advice is clear. There are moments when it is better to leave the problem alone for a while. Allowing an interval of rest to the problem and to yourself, you may obtain more tomorrow with less effort.

Past ages regarded a sudden good idea as an inspiration, a gift of the gods. You must deserve such a gift by work, or at least by a fervent wish.

The theory of subconscious work is debatable; some psychologists have discovered the beginnings of alternative explanations. But the practical advice is not debatable. There are moments when continued conscious effort is counterproductive, and the wisest move is to stop — having first earned the right to stop by genuine work.


## The heuristic syllogism

The reasoning behind signs of progress deserves a closer look, because it is the simplest and most widespread pattern of plausible reasoning — the kind of reasoning that is not proof but is indispensable to discovery.

The pattern:

$$
\begin{array}{l}
\text{If } A \text{ is true, then } B \text{ is also true, as we know.} \\
\text{Now, it turns out that } B \text{ is true.} \\
\hline
\text{Therefore, } A \text{ becomes more credible.}
\end{array}
$$

Compare this with the demonstrative pattern (modus tollens):

$$
\begin{array}{l}
\text{If } A \text{ then } B \\
B \text{ false} \\
\hline
A \text{ false}
\end{array}
$$

The demonstrative pattern is conclusive. If $A$ implies $B$ and $B$ is false, then $A$ is false — period. The heuristic pattern is not conclusive. If $A$ implies $B$ and $B$ is true, $A$ is *more credible* — but it may still be false. Columbus saw birds many times and was disappointed. Just once came the day when the signs multiplied and were followed by land.

**Direction versus magnitude.** The *direction* of the heuristic conclusion is determined by the premises: $A$ becomes more credible, not less. For any reasonable person, the premises involve that $A$ becomes more credible (certainly not less credible). But the *magnitude* is not determined. How *much* more credible? That depends on your background, your other evidence, your experience — things not captured in the two premises alone. This is why two reasonable people can read the same sign and draw different degrees of confidence from it. My neighbor and I can honestly disagree how much more credible $A$ becomes, since our temperaments, our backgrounds, and our unstated reasons may be different.

**The visible and invisible basis.** In the demonstrative syllogism the premises constitute a *full basis* on which the conclusion rests. If both premises stand, the conclusion stands too. If you receive some new information that does not change your belief in the premises, it cannot change your belief in the conclusion.

In the heuristic syllogism the premises constitute only one *part of the basis* — the fully expressed, the "visible" part. There is an unexpressed, invisible part, formed by inarticulate feelings perhaps, or by unstated reasons. It can happen that you receive new information that leaves your belief in both premises completely intact, but influences the trust you put in $A$ in a way just opposite to that expressed in the conclusion. The conclusion may be shaken and even overturned completely by commotions in the invisible parts of its foundation, although the premises, the visible part, stand quite firm.

This is not a defect. It is the nature of discovery. You work with plausible reasoning until you can replace it with proof. The heuristic syllogism is the scaffolding; the proof is the building. You need the scaffolding to erect the building, and you take it down when the building stands.

Should you trust heuristic conclusions? Should you follow them? Follow, but keep your eyes open. Trust, but look. And never renounce your judgment. If you take a heuristic conclusion as certain, you may be fooled and disappointed; but if you neglect heuristic conclusions altogether you will make no progress at all. The most important signs of progress are heuristic.

To find $A$ more plausible on the ground of the premises of your heuristic syllogism is only reasonable. Yet tomorrow you may find grounds, not interfering at all with these premises, that make $A$ appear less plausible, or even definitively refute it. This is the everyday experience of problem-solving: a line of attack that looked promising yesterday collapses today because of a new piece of information — yet the signs that originally encouraged you were perfectly real and remain perfectly valid. They just turned out to be outweighed.

The signs that convince the inventor that an idea is good, the circumstantial evidence of the lawyer, the inductive evidence of the scientist, statistical evidence invoked in many and diverse subjects — all these kinds of evidence agree in two essential points. First, they do not have the certainty of a strict demonstration. Second, they are useful in acquiring essentially new knowledge, and even indispensable to any not purely mathematical or logical knowledge.

Heuristic reasoning is good in itself. What is bad is to mix up heuristic reasoning with rigorous proof. What is worse is to sell heuristic reasoning for rigorous proof. The teaching of certain subjects could be essentially improved if the nature of heuristic reasoning were better understood, both its advantages and its limitations openly recognized, and if heuristic arguments were presented openly, with taste and frankness. A heuristic argument so presented may be useful; it may prepare for the rigorous argument of which it usually contains certain germs. But a heuristic argument is likely to be harmful if it is presented ambiguously with visible hesitation between shame and pretension.


## How signs help

You have a plan. You see pretty clearly where to begin and which steps to take first. But you do not quite see the layout of the road farther on; you are not quite certain your plan will work; and you have still a long way to go. So you start out cautiously in the direction your plan indicates and keep a lookout for signs of progress.

If the signs are rare or indistinct, you become more hesitant. If for a long time they fail to appear altogether, you may lose courage, turn back, and try another road. On the other hand, if the signs become more frequent as you proceed, if they multiply, your hesitation fades, your spirits rise, and you move with increasing confidence — just as Columbus and his companions did before sighting the island of San Salvador.

But if you fail to make progress, your attention falters, your interest fades, you get tired of the problem, your thoughts begin to wander, and there is danger of losing the problem altogether. To escape from this danger you have to set yourself a new question about the problem. A new question unfolds untried possibilities of contact with your previous knowledge, it revives your hope of making useful contacts. The new question reconquers your interest by varying the problem, by showing some new aspect of it.

Signs may guide your acts. Their absence may warn you of a blind alley and save you time and useless exertion; their presence may cause you to concentrate your effort upon the right spot.

Here is a practical rhythm. When you are past the understanding phase and working toward a plan or working through one, pause periodically and ask:

- Am I using more of the data than I was ten minutes ago?
- Has the problem become simpler or clearer?
- Have I connected the problem to something I already know?
- Can I foresee the shape of the solution — not the details, just the shape?
- Am I repeating myself — have the last three things I tried been essentially the same idea in different clothing?
- Has something surprised me? Surprise often signals contact with the real structure of the problem. A calculation that comes out unexpectedly clean, a case that behaves differently from the others, a symmetry you did not expect — these are worth investigating.

The main advantage of the expert may consist in a sort of extraordinary mental sensibility — with exquisite sensibility, the expert feels subtle signs of progress or notices their absence where the less experienced are unable to perceive a difference. The expert hunter notices traces of game and appraises even their freshness or staleness where the inexperienced one sees nothing.

This sensibility is not a talent you either have or lack. It develops with practice. Each problem you solve — and especially each problem you struggle with before solving — sharpens your sense of what approaching-the-solution looks like. The feelings become calibrated. What once felt like undifferentiated confusion gradually acquires texture: this kind of stuck feeling means you are missing a definition; that kind means you need an auxiliary problem; another kind means you should rest. The repertoire of felt signs grows with experience, and so does your accuracy in reading them.

Heuristic reasons are important although they prove nothing. To clarify your heuristic reasons is also important although behind any reason clarified there are many others that remain obscure and are perhaps still more important.


## Questions to keep

These questions distill the chapter. Return to them whenever you pause mid-problem:

- *Have I brought a new datum or a new clause of the condition into play since my last pause?*
- *Can I state the problem more simply now than I could before?*
- *Is my guess consistent with all the cases I have checked? Have I checked a boundary case?*
- *Am I persisting with a plan that still has untried potential, or am I repeating a failed approach?*
- *Have I worked hard enough to earn a rest — and if so, have I written down where I am before stopping?*
- *Does this approach feel well-balanced, or does something feel off? If so, what?*
- *Can I foresee the shape of the solution — induction, bijection, construction, contradiction?*
- *Have I connected the problem to something I already know — a related problem, a known theorem, a familiar technique?*
- *Has something surprised me? If so, is the surprise pointing at the real structure of the problem?*
- *Is my determination proportionate to my outlook — am I throwing in effort where the promise justifies it, or grinding where it does not?*
- *What sign would I expect to see next if I were on the right track?*
