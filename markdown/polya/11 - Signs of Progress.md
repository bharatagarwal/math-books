# Signs of Progress

As Columbus and his companions sailed westward across an unknown ocean they were cheered whenever they saw birds. They regarded a bird as a favorable sign, indicating the nearness of land. But in this they were repeatedly disappointed. They watched for other signs too. They thought that floating seaweed or low banks of cloud might indicate land, but they were again disappointed. One day, however, the signs multiplied. On Thursday, the 11th of October, 1492, "they saw sandpipers, and a green reed near the ship. Those of the caravel *Pinta* saw a cane and a pole, and they took up another small pole which appeared to have been worked by iron; also another bit of cane, a land-plant, and a small board. The crew of the caravel *Niña* also saw signs of land, and a small branch covered with berries. Everyone breathed afresh and rejoiced at these signs." And the next day they sighted land.

You are not crossing an ocean. You are solving a problem. But the experience is the same: you work intensely, you watch for signs that you are getting somewhere, and you need to read those signs honestly. Some will mislead you. Some will save you. This chapter is about telling them apart.


## What progress feels like

Progress is not a single event. It is a change in your conception of the problem. At the outset you have the bare statement. By the end you have the solution. Between those two points, your understanding deepens in ways you can sometimes feel before you can articulate.

Polya identified four aspects of this deepening:

**Mobilization.** You extract from memory the facts, theorems, and formerly solved problems that bear on the current one. Each time a recall question (*Have you seen it before? Do you know a related problem?*) produces a useful answer, you have mobilized something.

**Organization.** You combine what you have recalled into a structure adapted to the problem. You are not collecting facts; you are fitting them together. The structuring questions drive this: *Can you restate the problem? Can you introduce suitable notation?*

**Reconception.** Enriched with recalled material and reshaped by your attempts, the problem looks different now than it did at the start. You see new aspects, new connections. Your mode of conception has changed.

**Prevision.** You begin to foresee the shape of the solution: which tools will matter, which steps will come, how the argument will close.

These four advance together, sometimes steadily, sometimes in jumps. A sudden jump is what people call a bright idea: an abrupt reorganization of your outlook, a confident prevision of the steps ahead. You cannot force a bright idea. But you can monitor whether the conditions for one are building.


## Clearly expressible signs

Certain mental operations are typically useful in solving problems. When such an operation succeeds, its success is felt as a sign of progress. Each question on the list, appropriately read, is also a sign of progress: if the question produces an answer, you have advanced.

**A new datum comes into play.** You have been working with some of the data but not all of it. Suddenly you see how to involve a datum you had not yet used. In a well-posed problem, every datum matters. If a new move brings an unused datum into the picture, that move has something in common with the correct solution.

Suppose you are counting the subsets of an $n$-element set. You notice that each element contributes an independent binary choice — in or out — and that this accounts for *every* element. You have brought all the data into play at once. That is a very good sign.

**A clause of the condition is satisfied.** The condition of your problem may have several parts. Each part you manage to satisfy is progress. If you conjecture that the number of subsets of $\{1, 2, \ldots, n\}$ is $2^n$, you can check: does this give $1$ when $n = 0$? It does. Does it give $2$ when $n = 1$? It does. Each verified case is a clause of the condition satisfied.

**A simpler related problem emerges.** If, while struggling with a problem, a simpler analogous problem presents itself, spontaneously, not by forced search, that is a favorable sign. The simpler problem may serve as a stepping stone or may contain the germ of the method you need.

**Your notation starts to work for you.** When the symbols you chose begin to reveal structure you did not put there deliberately, something is going right. If you represent subsets as binary strings of length $n$ and then notice that the strings are just the binary representations of $0$ through $2^n - 1$, the notation has done work for you.

**The problem becomes easier to state.** If after some work you can restate the problem more simply, more precisely, or in a form closer to problems you have solved before, you have advanced.

**You see what kind of argument will close it.** Even before you have the details, if you can foresee that the proof will go by induction, or by bijection, or by contradiction, you have a plan outline. Plans are worth more than isolated moves.

**You recall a related problem that was solved before.** This may be the most decisive move of all. A formerly solved problem related to yours, by sharing the unknown, or the data, or the method, is a potential bridge.

To read such signs no occult science is needed, only a little common sense and a little experience.


## Less clearly expressible signs

Not all signs come labeled. When you work intently, you feel keenly the pace of your progress (elated when it is rapid, depressed when it is slow) without being able to point out any distinct sign.

"It looks good to me," say the unsophisticated. More sophisticated people express themselves with nuance: "This is a well-balanced plan," or "No, something is still lacking and that spoils the harmony." Behind these expressions there is an unmistakable feeling which you follow with confidence and which leads you frequently in the right direction. If such feeling is very strong and emerges suddenly, you speak of inspiration. People usually cannot doubt their inspirations and are sometimes fooled by them.

What is the nature of these guiding feelings? The clearly expressible signs are connected with the success or failure of definite mental operations. The less clearly expressible feelings may be similarly connected with more obscure mental activities, activities whose nature is more "psychological" and less "logical." You cannot always say *why* the approach feels right. But the feeling is real, and often correct.

Treat these feelings as you treat the clearer signs: with serious attention, not blind obedience. The right response: always follow your inspiration, with a grain of doubt.


## Misleading signs

Not every encouraging feeling is a real sign. Some signs are noise.

**Early fluency.** You set up notation quickly, draw a figure, restate the problem in two ways. You feel productive. But you have not yet engaged the hard part. Fluency in the setup phase is expected and says little about whether you will find the key idea. Do not mistake activity for progress.

**A long calculation that "feels like work."** Computation is sometimes necessary but never sufficient. If you are deep in algebraic manipulation without a clear reason, without a plan that told you *why* this calculation matters, you may be grinding without advancing. Ask: *Why am I doing this particular calculation? What will I learn from the answer?*

**A result that is "almost right."** Your conjecture works for $n = 1, 2, 3$ but fails for $n = 4$. The temptation is to patch the formula. But often "almost right" means your approach has a structural flaw, and patching will only hide it. Before you patch, ask: *Why did it fail? Is the failure accidental or does it reveal a misunderstanding?*

**Premature conviction.** You guess an answer and immediately believe it. Polya warned about this most forcefully. "Your guess may be right, but it is foolish to accept a vivid guess as a proven truth — as primitive people often do. Your guess may be wrong. But it is also foolish to disregard a vivid guess altogether — as pedantic people sometimes do." The right response to a guess is to *examine* it, not to adopt it or discard it.

**Pattern without mechanism.** You observe a numerical pattern (the first few values are $1, 2, 5, 14, 42$) and you feel certain the pattern will continue. But unless you can articulate *why* the pattern holds, you have an observation, not understanding.

**Ignoring unfavorable signs.** The reverse error: you see signs that your approach is failing and you explain them away. The formula does not work for $n = 4$ but you decide the case is "degenerate." The proof requires an assumption you cannot justify but you press on. This is Mr. John Jones, clinging to a hypothesis against the evidence.

Signs may also deceive in a subtler way. Polya confessed: "I once abandoned a certain path for lack of signs, but a man who came after me and followed that path a little farther made an important discovery — to my great annoyance and long-lasting regret. He not only had more perseverance than I did but he also read correctly a certain sign which I had failed to notice."

Yes, signs may misguide you in any single case, but they guide you right in the majority of them. A hunter may misinterpret now and then the traces of his game but he must be right on the average, otherwise he could not make a living by hunting. The expert problem-solver knows by experience how the situation looks and feels when the solution is near. The main advantage of the expert may consist in such knowledge.


## How signs help

You have a plan. You see pretty clearly where to begin. But you do not quite see the layout of the road farther on. So you start out cautiously and keep a lookout for signs.

If the signs are rare or indistinct, you become more hesitant. If for a long time they fail to appear altogether, you may lose courage and try another road. If the signs multiply, your hesitation fades and you move with increasing confidence, just as Columbus and his companions did before sighting San Salvador.

Signs may guide your acts. Their absence may warn you of a blind alley and save you time. Their presence may cause you to concentrate your effort upon the right spot.

Here is a practical check. When you are past the understanding phase and working toward a plan, pause and ask:

- Am I using more of the data than I was ten minutes ago?
- Has the problem become simpler or clearer?
- Have I connected the problem to something I already know?
- Can I foresee the shape of the solution, not the details, just the shape?
- Am I repeating myself? Have the last three things I tried been essentially the same idea in different clothing?
- Has something surprised me?

Surprise often signals contact with the real structure of the problem. A calculation that comes out unexpectedly clean, a case that behaves differently from the others, a symmetry you did not expect — these are worth investigating.

This sensibility is not a talent you either have or lack. It develops with practice. Each problem you solve — and especially each problem you struggle with before solving — sharpens your sense of what approaching-the-solution looks like. The repertoire of felt signs grows with experience, and so does your accuracy in reading them.


## Questions to keep

- *Have I brought a new datum or a new clause of the condition into play since my last pause?*
- *Can I state the problem more simply now than I could before?*
- *Am I repeating a failed approach, or has something genuinely changed since my last attempt?*
- *Has something surprised me, and if so, is the surprise pointing at the real structure of the problem?*
